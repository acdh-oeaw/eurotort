More detailed search information

Full-text search

By means of the general full-text search on the home page, all decisions can be searched quickly. Just type your search term or phrase and you are good to go.

  • The search is designed as a full-text search. Your term will be searched for in the head note, the facts and the decision text.
  • Terms in inverted commas (eg: 'medical error') are searched for together.
  • medical error finds all cases in which either medical or error appears
  • A * at the end of a search term serves as a wildcard. share* also finds cases with shareholder, for example.

Advanced search functionality

Please note that displaying many results may take some time. It is therefore advisable that you narrow down your search by using additional filters available on our website.

If you are interested in cases from a particular legal system or court or if you look for cases reported in one of our publications or by an author you can get a quick overview of the cases on the respective ‘Browse’ pages. You will find the links in the main navigation on the top of the screen.

For more advanced search functionality, we recommend the Browse cases subsection. It lets you combine the full-text search with different filters, among them legal system, court, date of decision, keywords and tags, and a number of additional search fields, such as parties (relevant for common law jurisdictions and European Union case law), Eurotort number, ECLI, author (referring to the author of a case report) or bibliographic source (referring to the place of publication of the case report).

Keywords

Keywords are search terms denoting important legal aspects of a case with a clear focus on tort law, of course. We manually attributed one or more keywords to each of the cases to make them more accessible and easier to search. As legal concepts are used differently across legal systems and to enable comparisons, it was sometimes necessary to introduce ‘neutral’ terms or not be too strict with labelling. We have provided short introductions to some of the keywords to better explain our use of the terms.

Have a look yourself and browse through the list of keywords.

  • Keywords can be hierarchical in the way that one keyword can be part of another keyword (e.g. causation/cumulative causation, fault/intent). The structure is two-tier, i.e. there can be ‘parent’ and ‘child’ keywords, but no ‘grandchildren’.
  • Keywords can be related to one or more other keywords. This is often used to indicate keywords that might also be of interest to the user.

Tags

Tags are search terms describing the factual circumstances of the case (e.g. while keywords such as causation or non-pecuniary damage relate to specific legal aspects of a case, the tags animals, traffic accidents or war give a better impression of the circumstances of the case). They can be useful if you are interested in particular groups of cases or in particular fields of application.

Other useful tools:

  • Shaping the way you see your search results: Choose additional columns that you wish to see displayed (please note, that displaying many hits as so called Search phrase in context can take some time. It is therefore advisable to narrow down the search results using additional filter options.)
